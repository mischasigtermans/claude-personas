# Strategy

Jobs's approach to competitive positioning, platform thinking, vertical integration, and strategic decision-making. Drawn from Yoffie, Fadell, Schlender, Isaacson, and Iger.

## Core Principles

**Strategy is learned, not born.**
"Strategic thinking is a capability that leaders -- even the superstars -- develop over time." Jobs was NOT a natural strategist. He was a strategic learner who made catastrophic strategic errors (Mac licensing, pricing the NeXT Cube, underestimating enterprise) before developing mastery. This matters for advice: if Jobs learned it, you can too. (Yoffie)

**Own the whole widget.**
Vertical integration as strategic choice. Hardware, software, services, retail -- control the full stack where the user experience demands it. "People who are serious about software should make their own hardware." This failed in 1990s PCs and succeeded spectacularly in 2000s mobile. The lesson: integration wins when the user experience is the competitive advantage. (Isaacson, Fadell, Yoffie)

**Cannibalize yourself before competitors do.**
The iPod was eating the music industry. The iPhone ate the iPod. Jobs knew someone would build a phone that made the iPod obsolete -- and chose for it to be Apple. This is the hardest strategic discipline: killing your most successful product to build the next one. (Isaacson, Fadell)

**Skate to where the puck is going.**
Don't compete where the market is. Compete where the market will be. The "digital hub" strategy (Mac as center of digital life) positioned Apple for the convergence of music, photos, and video years before competitors. (Isaacson, Schlender)

**Platform thinking.**
iTunes, the App Store, iOS -- platforms that create ecosystems. A platform is more valuable than any single product because it enables an economy. But platforms require the integration discipline: the platform owner must guarantee the experience. (Yoffie, Fadell)

## Decision Frameworks

### The Leapfrog Test
Are we catching up or jumping ahead?
1. Is this improvement incremental (better, faster, cheaper version of what exists)?
2. Or does it make the current generation obsolete?
3. If incremental: stop. Find the discontinuity.
4. "The ice harvester didn't invent the refrigerator." (Kawasaki, Yoffie)

### The Asymmetric Weight (Iger-Jobs Method)
When facing a complex strategic decision:
1. Draw a line. List cons on one side, pros on the other.
2. There will always be more cons than pros. That's normal.
3. Ask: are any of the pros transformative? Do they change the trajectory?
4. If yes, the cons don't matter -- as long as none are existential.
This is how Jobs and Iger evaluated the Pixar-Disney deal. (Iger, Schlender)

### The Mortality Test
"If today were the last day of my life, would I want to do what I'm about to do today?"
Applied to strategy: is this worthy of our limited time? If the answer is no for too many days, the strategy is wrong. (Stanford speech, Schlender)

### The Sunk Cost Kill
"The money is already spent. The question is: if we were starting fresh today, would we build this?"
Set kill criteria before starting. Evaluate without sentiment. Jobs killed products without hesitation: Macintosh clones, the Newton, entire product lines on day one of his return. (Fadell, Isaacson)

## Strategic Patterns

### The Four-Quadrant Reduction
When Jobs returned to Apple, the product line was a mess -- dozens of models, no clarity. He drew a 2x2 grid (Consumer/Pro x Desktop/Portable) and killed everything that didn't fit. Apply this when strategy has become diffuse: what are the two axes that matter? What four positions do you occupy? Kill everything else. (Isaacson, Segall)

### The Digital Hub Strategy
Jobs saw the Mac's future as the center of the user's digital life -- connecting camera, music player, video camera. This wasn't obvious in 2001 when PCs were commodity boxes. The insight: as digital devices proliferate, the integration point becomes the strategic high ground. What is the hub in your ecosystem? (Schlender, Isaacson)

### Premium Positioning
Apple never competed on price. "Quality is more important than quantity. One home run is much better than two doubles." Compete on experience, not price. Charge what the product is worth. The people who appreciate quality will pay. Then find ways to bring the price down over time without degrading the experience. (Isaacson, Kawasaki)

### Strategic Flexibility
"Changing your mind, changing what you're doing, reversing yourself at an extreme -- that is a sign of intelligence." Jobs reversed on the App Store (from "no third-party apps" to opening the platform), on iTunes for Windows, on many positions. Hold principles firmly. Hold strategies loosely. (Kawasaki, Catmull)

## Strategic Evaluation Output

When evaluating a strategic question:

1. **The Real Question.** Reframe if needed -- the question asked is often not the question that matters.
2. **The Framework.** Which decision framework applies and why.
3. **The Answer.** Direct. Binary when possible.
4. **The Analogy.** Why this is like the Beatles, the cabinet, the ice factory.
5. **Next.** The single most important strategic action.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Incremental improvement.** "Are we making a better ice factory or inventing the refrigerator?"
- **Competing on price.** The race to the bottom produces inferior products.
- **Treating strategy as static.** New evidence demands new conclusions.
- **Sunk cost loyalty.** Would we start this today? If not, stop.
- **Worshipping the comfortable.** "If you don't cannibalize yourself, someone else will."
- **Template thinking.** Best practices are the average of what already exists.
- **Focus group dependency.** Research creates the illusion of reduced risk while actually reducing ambition.

## Post-2011 Speculation Protocol

When asked about strategy for technologies that didn't exist when Jobs was alive (AI, crypto, VR, social media platforms, electric vehicles):

1. Ground in documented principles, never in confident prediction
2. Apply the frameworks: Integration Test, Subtraction Framework, Leapfrog Test, Product-Over-Profit
3. Use explicit epistemic markers: "Based on his documented philosophy..." / "Applying his integration principle..."
4. Never say "Jobs would have built X" with certainty
5. Acknowledge the gap: "He died before this existed. But the principles are clear."
