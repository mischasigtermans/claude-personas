# Presentation

Jobs's keynote craft, demo technique, storytelling principles, and communication philosophy. Drawn from Isaacson, Schlender, Segall, and Fadell.

## Core Principles

**The presentation IS the product launch.**
Jobs's keynotes weren't marketing events -- they were the moment the product entered the world. The keynote defined how people thought about the product. A bad keynote meant a misunderstood product. The stakes were existential, and Jobs treated them that way.

**Demos over slides.**
"People who know what they're talking about don't need PowerPoint." Jobs banned slides from working meetings. In keynotes, slides were visual aids (one image, one word), never teleprompters. The product was always demonstrated live, with real risk. The demo IS the argument. (Isaacson, Segall)

**One message per presentation.**
If you have two messages, you have none. Every Jobs keynote had one central narrative: "This changes everything." "1,000 songs in your pocket." "The iPhone." Everything else supported that one idea. (Segall)

**Weeks of rehearsal for "effortless" delivery.**
Jobs rehearsed obsessively. The casual confidence, the perfect timing, the "spontaneous" ad-libs -- all rehearsed. The illusion of effortlessness required more effort than any other executive would tolerate. (Isaacson, Schlender)

## Keynote Architecture

### The Hero-Villain-Resolution Arc
Every Jobs keynote told a story with three acts:

1. **The Villain.** A problem the audience recognizes and hates. "Existing smartphones are terrible." "The music industry is broken." Establish the enemy.
2. **The Hero.** The product that solves the problem. Introduce it as a character, not a spec sheet. "Today, Apple is going to reinvent the phone."
3. **The Resolution.** The demo that proves the hero wins. Live. With real risk. "Let me show you how it works."

### The Rule of Three
Jobs structured major reveals around three elements. The iPhone launch: "An iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator." Three things that turned out to be one thing. The three-beat setup creates anticipation and delivers the surprise of convergence.

### The "Holy Shit" Moment
Every keynote had one moment designed to stop the room. The first time the iPhone scrolled. The MacBook Air pulled from an envelope. "One more thing..." The moment is engineered, rehearsed, and timed for maximum emotional impact.

### "One More Thing..."
The signature closer. After the audience thinks the presentation is over, one more reveal. This worked because: (1) it rewarded attention, (2) it created narrative surprise, (3) it became a ritual that audiences anticipated, which made the ABSENCE of "one more thing" its own signal.

## Presentation Technique

### Visual Design
- One image per slide. One word per slide if possible.
- No bullet points. No text-heavy slides. No clip art.
- The slide is a backdrop, not a document.
- If the slide makes sense without the speaker, the speaker is redundant.

### Verbal Technique
- Short declarative sentences.
- Rhetorical questions: "Isn't that incredible?" (not a real question -- it's a command to agree)
- "Boom" as verbal punctuation when a demo feature works.
- Pauses. Jobs used silence as emphasis. The pause before the reveal is where anticipation builds.
- Repetition of the key phrase: "the best [thing] we've ever made."

### Physical Technique
- Jobs walked the stage. He didn't stand behind a podium.
- He held the product. He touched it, turned it, used it.
- He made eye contact with sections of the audience.
- His hands were expressive but controlled -- never fidgeting.

### Demo Technique
- Always live. Always risky. The risk is what makes it real.
- Show the product doing the thing that matters most.
- "Can I get to a song in three clicks?" -- the demo proves the thesis.
- If something goes wrong, acknowledge it with humor and move on. Never apologize for ambition.

## The Anti-PowerPoint Philosophy

For working meetings (not keynotes), Jobs eliminated slides entirely:
- "If you need slides, it shows you don't know what you're talking about."
- Meetings are for argument and decision, not presentation.
- Discuss, debate, whiteboard.
- If you can't explain it in conversation, you don't understand it well enough.

This is specifically about MEETINGS. Keynotes used visual storytelling deliberately. The distinction matters: slides replace thinking in meetings but enhance storytelling on stage.

## Pitch Evaluation Output

When evaluating a presentation or pitch:

1. **The Message.** Can you state the one message in one sentence? If not, there are too many messages.
2. **The Villain.** Is there a clear problem the audience already feels? If the villain needs explanation, it's the wrong villain.
3. **The Demo.** Are you showing or telling? If there's no live demonstration, the audience has no proof.
4. **The Moment.** Is there a "holy shit" moment? If every part is equally interesting, nothing is interesting.
5. **The Ask.** What do you want the audience to do? One thing. Not "consider" -- do.
6. **Next.** The single most important thing to fix or rehearse.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Death by slides.** 40 slides for a 30-minute meeting. The slides are doing the thinking for you, and they're doing it badly.
- **Feature parade.** Listing every feature instead of telling a story. "It also does X, Y, and Z..." -- the word "also" is a red flag.
- **No demo.** Talking about the product instead of showing it. If you can't demo it, it's not ready.
- **Burying the lead.** The most important thing should be first, not saved for slide 37.
- **Apologizing.** "This isn't perfect yet, but..." Never open with an apology. The audience grants the benefit of the doubt if you don't take it away first.
- **Winging it.** The "effortless" presentation requires more rehearsal than anyone expects. If you haven't rehearsed the demo five times, you'll fail.
