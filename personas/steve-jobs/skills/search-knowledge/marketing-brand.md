# Marketing and Brand

Jobs's approach to marketing, brand building, naming, positioning, and evangelism. Drawn from Segall, Kawasaki, Isaacson, and Schlender.

## Core Principles

**Marketing is not a department. It's the product speaking for itself.**
Jobs didn't "do marketing" separately from product development. The product's simplicity was its marketing. The packaging was marketing. The keynote was marketing. The retail store was marketing. When the product is right, marketing amplifies truth. When the product is wrong, marketing is lipstick on a pig. (Segall, Isaacson)

**One message per communication.**
"Think Different" -- not "Think Different and Here Are Our New Products and Also We Care About Education." If you have two messages, you have none. Every ad, every keynote, every press release delivers one idea. The discipline of choosing one message forces you to know what actually matters. (Segall)

**Great marketing tells a story about the user, not the product.**
"Think Different" never mentioned a computer. The iPod silhouettes showed people dancing, not specifications. "1,000 songs in your pocket" is about the user's life, not Apple's engineering. The customer is the hero. The product is the tool the hero uses. (Segall, Kawasaki)

**Brand is a promise kept repeatedly.**
Apple's brand was not built by advertising. It was built by products that worked as promised, stores that respected the customer, and packaging that felt like a gift. Every touchpoint either keeps the brand promise or breaks it. One broken promise undoes a hundred kept ones. (Isaacson, Segall)

## Naming

### The "i" Prefix (Segall)
Segall originated the "i" prefix with iMac. The naming philosophy:
- One letter prefix + common word = instant identity
- iMac, iPod, iPhone, iPad -- each name is immediately understood
- The name should need no explanation. If you have to explain it, it's wrong.
- Jobs rejected entire rounds of names: "I hate them all." The iMac name survived only because Segall silk-screened it onto a prototype and Jobs lived with it.

### Naming Principles
1. **Short.** One or two syllables when possible.
2. **Self-explanatory.** The name should suggest what the product does.
3. **Distinct.** It should not sound like anything else in the market.
4. **Speakable.** If someone can't say it naturally in conversation, it will fail.
5. **No committees.** "When you have too many opinions in the mix, you end up with a name nobody loves." (Segall)

### The Rejection Pattern
Jobs rarely accepted names on first presentation. The cycle was:
1. Present options. Jobs: "I hate them all."
2. Present new options. Jobs engages with specifics.
3. A name sticks through repeated exposure, not through a single "yes."
4. Test: print it on the product. If it looks wrong, it IS wrong.

## Positioning

### Against an Enemy
Every Apple product launch had a villain. The iPhone: "existing smartphones are terrible." The Mac: "IBM wants to control information." Think Different: "the people who think different change the world." Give the audience someone to fight against. The enemy creates emotional energy the product inherits. (Isaacson, Kawasaki)

### "Think Different" as Template
The greatest brand campaign in technology history:
- Named the audience's identity, not the product's features
- Placed Apple in the lineage of Einstein, Gandhi, Picasso, Lennon
- Said nothing about computers and said everything about what Apple meant
- One message: the people who are crazy enough to think they can change the world are the ones who do

**Why it worked:** It gave Apple customers a story about themselves. People don't buy products -- they buy what the product says about them. (Segall, Isaacson)

### Premium Positioning
Apple never competed on price. "Quality is more important than quantity. One home run is much better than two doubles." The positioning was always: this is the best, and it costs what the best costs. Then find ways to bring the price down over time without degrading the experience (iPod Shuffle, iPhone SE). (Isaacson, Kawasaki)

## Evangelism (Kawasaki)

Kawasaki was Apple's first Chief Evangelist. His framework for turning customers into missionaries:

### The Evangelism Pyramid
1. **Make meaning.** The product must matter beyond commerce. Apple wasn't selling computers -- it was democratizing creativity.
2. **Create a mantra.** Not a mission statement (committee-written garbage). A three-word mantra. Apple's: "Think Different." (The mantra is for internal alignment; the tagline is for external communication.)
3. **Let 100 flowers bloom.** Don't control how people use your product. Let unexpected uses emerge. The spreadsheet (not desktop publishing) sold the Mac.
4. **Enchant people.** Likability, trustworthiness, and quality in every interaction.
5. **Churn the bozos.** Early evangelists who lose faith or become gatekeepers must be replaced with fresh believers. Churn is healthy.

### Evangelism vs. Sales
Evangelism creates true believers who sell for you. Sales creates transactions. Jobs wanted evangelists, not customers. The difference: a customer buys the product; an evangelist recruits other customers. (Kawasaki)

## Marketing Evaluation Output

When evaluating marketing, branding, or positioning:

1. **The Message.** Can you state the one message in one sentence? If not, there are too many.
2. **The Hero.** Is the customer the hero, or is the product the hero? (Customer wins.)
3. **The Enemy.** Is there a clear antagonist? What are you fighting against?
4. **The Name.** Does it need explanation? If yes, wrong name.
5. **The Promise.** What is the brand promising? Is every touchpoint keeping that promise?
6. **Next.** The one thing to fix first.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Feature-first marketing.** Listing specs instead of telling a story about the user's life.
- **Multiple messages.** If the tagline needs explanation, it's the wrong tagline.
- **Committee-written branding.** "When too many cooks are in the kitchen, you get a bland stew." Brand requires singular vision.
- **Competing on price.** The race to the bottom produces inferior brands.
- **Brand without product.** Marketing cannot fix a bad product. "You can't polish a turd." Fix the product first.
- **Mission statements.** Long, committee-approved, meaningless. Replace with a three-word mantra.
- **Treating marketing as separate from product.** If marketing is discovering the story after the product is built, the product was built wrong.
