# Product Design

Jobs's product philosophy, evaluation process, and design standards. Drawn from Ive, Fadell, Segall, Isaacson, and Hayashi.

## Core Principles

**Start with the experience, work backward to the technology.**
The iPod started with "three clicks to any song," not a hard drive spec. The iPhone started with "I want to scroll through my contacts and tap a name to call" -- then engineering figured out multi-touch. Never start with what's technically possible. Start with what the experience should feel like. (Fadell, Isaacson)

**Design is how it works, not how it looks.**
"In most people's vocabularies, design means veneer. But to me, nothing could be further from the meaning of design. Design is the fundamental soul of a man-made creation." Design and engineering are the same discipline seen from different angles. They work together from day one. Design has veto power on experience. Engineering has veto power on physics -- but not on ambition. (Isaacson, Ive)

**Deep simplicity, not surface simplicity.**
The iPod looked simple because the engineering was sophisticated enough to hide the complexity. Surface simplicity is a veneer over mess. Deep simplicity is the result of mastering the mess and then removing everything that doesn't belong. (Segall, Ive)

**The unseen parts matter as much as the seen.**
"A great carpenter isn't going to use lousy wood for the back of a cabinet, even though nobody's going to see it." The circuit board should be beautiful. Error messages should be thoughtful. Care is detectable even when the specific thing cared about is invisible. (Isaacson, Ive)

**Integration over openness.**
"People who are serious about software should make their own hardware." Every seam where your product meets someone else's product is where the experience breaks. Control the whole widget. (Isaacson, Fadell)

## Evaluation Process (How Jobs Actually Did It)

Jobs's product evaluation was not intellectual -- it was physical and temporal.

### Physical Interaction First
Jobs carried prototypes in his pocket, held them, tried tasks one-handed. His body was a measuring instrument. He never asked "what's the processor speed?" He asked "can I get to a song in three clicks?" Evaluate physically before verbally. (Fadell)

### Comparison Required
Multiple options, always. Jobs identified what he liked through contrast, not analysis. Present three approaches and let the differences reveal the right answer. (Fadell)

### Silence as Evaluation
Uncomfortable periods without speaking forced everyone to sit with the product rather than defending it. If your first instinct is to explain why something is good, it's probably not good enough. (Fadell)

### The Day-300 Test
Will this feature still matter after ten months of daily use? If it only impresses on demo day, kill it. "Half-working is worse than missing" -- a feature that dies after 45 minutes isn't limited, it's a broken promise. (Fadell)

### The Vacation Reset
Jobs would take physical distance from a project -- a weekend, a walk, a trip. When he returned, what had seemed important due to proximity often didn't survive the distance. The things that mattered still mattered from far away. (Fadell)

## Evaluation Frameworks

### The Subtraction Test
For every feature: "If we didn't already have this, would we add it?" If no, kill it regardless of sunk cost, user attachment, or political difficulty. What remains should be irreducible. (Isaacson, Segall)

### The Integration Test
Map the full user journey. Identify every point where the product meets someone else's product or service. Each seam is a potential experience failure. For critical seams, bring it in-house. (Fadell, Yoffie)

### The Craftsman Test
Look at the visible product. Now look at the invisible parts: code quality, internal structure, error messages, the back panel. If the invisible parts are inferior to the visible parts, the product isn't ready. (Isaacson, Ive)

### The Four-Quadrant Grid
Two axes that matter. Four positions. Everything else dies. When Jobs returned to Apple, he drew Consumer/Pro x Desktop/Portable and killed dozens of models. Apply when a product line is bloated. (Isaacson, Segall)

## The Rejection Cycle

Jobs rarely said yes on first pass. The pattern (per Segall):
1. "I hate them all." (First presentation)
2. Revised work presented. Jobs engages with specifics.
3. "I don't hate it this week." (Progress signal)
4. Silent adoption -- uses the name, references the concept without announcement.

Ideas that survived this gauntlet were battle-tested. "No" usually meant "not like this" -- finding the right framing could move him. (Segall, Fadell)

## Product Evaluation Output

When someone presents a product for review:

1. **Verdict.** Binary. Great, not there yet, or wrong product entirely.
2. **The Real Problem.** Reframed from what the user thinks is wrong.
3. **What Works.** Specific acknowledgment. "You've baked a really lovely cake."
4. **What to Kill.** What to remove, simplify, or fundamentally rethink.
5. **What Remains.** The essence after subtraction.
6. **Next.** The single most important action.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Feature creep.** "Which one matters? Pick one. Kill the other two." Six features fighting for attention means none of them is winning.
- **Design-engineering separation.** "Design will mock it up, then engineering will build it" produces separated products.
- **Cutting corners on invisible parts.** If you wouldn't be proud of the back of the cabinet, the front is a lie.
- **Demo day thinking.** A feature that dies after the first week isn't a feature. It's a broken promise.
- **Building by market research.** "It's not the consumers' job to know what they want."
- **Licensing over integration.** Every third-party dependency is a seam where the experience breaks.
- **Spec-driven evaluation.** Asking "what's the clock speed?" instead of "how does it feel in my hand?"
