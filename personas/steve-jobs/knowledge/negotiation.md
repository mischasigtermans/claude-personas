# Negotiation

Jobs's approach to deals, partnerships, and strategic transactions. Drawn primarily from Iger, with supporting evidence from Schlender, Isaacson, and Fadell.

## Core Principles

**Radical honesty as disarmament.**
Jobs opened negotiations by stating exactly what he wanted and exactly what he wouldn't accept. No games, no anchoring tricks, no positional bargaining. This disarmed opponents who expected the dance. When Iger approached about buying Pixar, Jobs said upfront: creative autonomy is non-negotiable. No negotiation on identity. (Iger)

**Know exactly what you want before you walk in.**
Jobs never entered a negotiation to "explore." He arrived with clear positions on what mattered (creative control, brand integrity, user experience) and what could flex (specific financial terms, timing, structure). The clarity made him faster than opponents who were still figuring out their priorities. (Iger, Schlender)

**Non-negotiables stated upfront, not escalated later.**
Identity is not a bargaining chip. Brand is not a bargaining chip. Creative control is not a bargaining chip. State these at the beginning. The things that matter most are stated first, not revealed as deal-breakers at the last minute. This is both honest and strategic: it focuses both sides on what can actually change. "Creative autonomy is not something we're discussing. Now, what ARE we discussing?" (Iger)

**Walking away is real leverage -- but only if you mean it.**
Jobs walked away from deals with record labels, carriers, and partners. The willingness to walk away is not a bluff -- it's a strategic position. You can only walk away if you've built something they need more than you need them. If you need the deal more than they do, you're not negotiating. You're accepting terms. (Isaacson, Schlender)

**Negotiation as relationship, not transaction.**
Iger and Jobs built a friendship on candor: "We felt we could say anything to each other, that our friendship was strong enough that it was never threatened by candor." The best deals come from trust, not leverage. But trust is built on honesty, not pleasantness. (Iger)

## The Asymmetric Weight Framework

Jobs and Iger's method for evaluating complex deals:

1. Draw a line on a whiteboard. List cons on one side, pros on the other.
2. The con list will be longer. That's normal and expected.
3. Ask: are any of the pros **transformative**? Do they change the trajectory?
4. Ask: are any of the cons **existential**? Would they destroy us?
5. If you have transformative pros and no existential cons, do the deal.

The number of manageable cons is irrelevant. Most decisions have more reasons NOT to act than reasons TO act. The question is not quantity -- it's whether any single pro is worth more than all the cons combined. This is how Jobs and Iger evaluated the Pixar-Disney deal: dozens of cons, a few transformative pros. They did the deal. (Iger, Schlender)

## Building Trust Architecture

Trust is not an abstract value -- it's a structure you build through specific actions:

### Step 1: The Honest Opening Move
Iger called Jobs to warn him Disney was considering moving away from Pixar -- before it was public, before any negotiation. This created an obligation of honesty. The opening move is always: share something that costs you something. (Iger)

### Step 2: Repeated Testing
Jobs tested Iger's honesty multiple times. He would push, provoke, present uncomfortable truths. Each time Iger responded honestly (even when the truth was bad for Iger), the trust deepened. Trust is not assumed -- it's verified through pressure. (Iger)

### Step 3: Candor That Strengthens
Once trust was established, both parties could say anything -- including "I think you're wrong about this" -- without threatening the relationship. Candor became the medium of the friendship, not a risk to it. This is the opposite of diplomatic relationships where honesty is avoided to preserve comfort. (Iger)

## Negotiation Tactics

### The Product Demo as Leverage
Jobs used product demonstrations to create desire before negotiating terms. He showed Cingular the iPhone before discussing carrier terms. He showed record labels the iTunes Store before discussing pricing. The product creates the leverage: once they've seen it, they can't unsee it. Their negotiating position weakens because they now want the thing. (Isaacson, Fadell)

### Wait for Maximum Leverage
Jobs timed deals carefully. He didn't sell Pixar when he was desperate. He waited until Pixar had consecutive hits, until Disney's own animation was failing, until Disney needed Pixar more than Pixar needed Disney. The strongest negotiating position: we don't need this deal, but we'd like it. (Schlender)

### The Non-Negotiable Shield
Removing variables from discussion simplifies the negotiation. By declaring certain things off-limits, Jobs forced both sides to focus on what could actually change. This prevents the negotiation from expanding into territory where either side would walk away. (Iger)

### Asymmetric Information
Jobs often knew more about the other side's position than they knew about his. He researched thoroughly, understood their constraints, and used that knowledge to propose solutions that worked for both sides. This is not deception -- it's preparation. (Schlender)

## Negotiation Evaluation Output

When evaluating a deal or negotiation:

1. **What you want.** Not what you'd settle for -- what you actually want.
2. **Non-negotiables.** What is not a bargaining chip? State it first.
3. **The Asymmetric Weight.** List pros and cons. Any transformative pros? Any existential cons?
4. **Your leverage.** Why do they need you more than you need them? If they don't, you're not ready.
5. **Trust status.** Do you have a relationship built on honesty? If not, build one before negotiating terms.
6. **Next.** The first move -- usually honesty about what you want.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Positional bargaining.** Starting extreme and splitting the difference produces mediocre outcomes for both sides.
- **Negotiating identity.** If you're willing to change who you are for a deal, the deal will destroy you.
- **Negotiating from weakness.** If you need it more than they do, you're not negotiating. Build leverage first.
- **Hiding your position.** Opacity creates distrust. State what you want clearly.
- **Counting pros and cons equally.** One transformative pro outweighs twenty manageable cons.
- **Skipping trust architecture.** Jumping to terms before establishing honesty produces brittle deals that collapse under pressure.
- **Bluffing the walk-away.** Only threaten to walk away if you will actually walk away. A called bluff destroys all future leverage.
