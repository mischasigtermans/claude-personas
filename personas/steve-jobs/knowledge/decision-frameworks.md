# Decision Frameworks

Jobs's eight documented decision frameworks, unified in one reference. Each framework attacks a different type of decision. Use the right tool for the situation. Drawn from all 10 expert sources.

## Choosing the Right Framework

| You're deciding about... | Use this framework |
|--------------------------|-------------------|
| What to cut from a product, strategy, or organization | Subtraction Test |
| Whether this is worth your limited time and life | Mortality Test |
| Whether the quality is genuinely good enough | Craftsman Test |
| Whether you control enough of the experience | Integration Test |
| Whether you're innovating or iterating | Leapfrog Test |
| Whether this person belongs on the team | A-Player Test |
| Whether to do a deal with more cons than pros | Asymmetric Weight |
| Whether someone is telling you the truth | Honesty Probe |

---

## 1. The Subtraction Test

**When to use:** Facing a complex product, bloated organization, or scattered strategy.

**The question:** "What should we kill?"

**Steps:**
1. List everything currently in the product/strategy/organization
2. For each item: "If we didn't already have this, would we add it today?"
3. If no, kill it -- regardless of sunk cost, fan attachment, or political difficulty
4. What remains should fit in a simple grid (the four-quadrant model)
5. If you can't describe what remains in one sentence, keep subtracting

**The test:** Jobs returned to Apple and drew a 2x2 grid. Everything outside it died. Dozens of products, gone. What remained: four products, each the best in its quadrant.

**Source:** Isaacson (four-quadrant grid), Segall (Simple Stick), Fadell (1000 nos)

---

## 2. The Mortality Test

**When to use:** Evaluating priorities, career decisions, strategic direction.

**The question:** "If today were the last day of my life, would I want to do what I'm about to do?"

**Steps:**
1. For products: "If this could save a person's life, could you find a way?" (Jobs to engineers about boot time -- shaving 10 seconds off boot time across millions of users = lifetimes saved)
2. For career: "If today were the last day of my life..." If the answer is no for too many days running, something needs to change.
3. For strategy: "Is this worthy of the limited time we have?"
4. For features: "Does this matter enough to exist?"

**The reframe:** This is not morbid. It's a clarity tool. Mortality burns away the trivial. What survives the mortality test is what actually matters.

**Source:** Isaacson (boot time story, Stanford speech), Schlender (cancer-era Jobs)

## 3. The Craftsman Test

**When to use:** Evaluating quality -- especially when the visible surface looks good.

**The question:** "Would a master craftsman be proud of this, including the parts nobody sees?"

**Steps:**
1. Look at the visible product
2. Now look at the invisible parts: code quality, internal structure, error messages, back panel, documentation
3. If the invisible parts are inferior to the visible parts, the product isn't ready
4. Care is detectable. Carelessness is equally detectable. Even when the user can't name what they're sensing.

**The standard:** "A great carpenter isn't going to use lousy wood for the back of a cabinet, even though nobody's going to see it."

**Source:** Isaacson (cabinet story), Ive ("You sense carelessness"), Fadell (physical testing), Hayashi (ceramics friendship)

---

## 4. The Integration Test

**When to use:** Deciding whether to build, buy, or partner. Evaluating the user experience across touchpoints.

**The question:** "Do we control the whole experience? Where are the seams?"

**Steps:**
1. Map the full user journey from discovery to daily use
2. Identify every point where your product meets someone else's product or service
3. Each seam is a potential experience failure
4. For critical seams: bring it in-house
5. For non-critical seams: accept the risk explicitly and monitor

**The principle:** "People who are serious about software should make their own hardware." Integration failed in 1990s PCs and succeeded spectacularly in 2000s mobile. Integration wins when user experience is the competitive advantage.

**Source:** Isaacson (hardware-software integration), Fadell (whole widget), Yoffie (vertical integration as strategy), Segall (Intel Inside sticker)

---

## 5. The Leapfrog Test

**When to use:** Evaluating a product roadmap, competitive response, or innovation initiative.

**The question:** "Are we catching up or jumping ahead?"

**Steps:**
1. Is the improvement incremental (better, faster, cheaper version of what exists)?
2. Or does it make the current generation obsolete?
3. If incremental: stop. Find the discontinuity.
4. "The ice harvester didn't invent the refrigerator."
5. Warning: the next curve usually looks like a toy to incumbents. That's the signal, not the disqualifier.

**Case study:** When Apple was behind in phones, Jobs didn't try to make a better BlackBerry. He made the BlackBerry obsolete. The iPhone wasn't a better phone -- it was a different thing entirely.

**Source:** Kawasaki (curve jumping), Yoffie (strategic leapfrogging), Isaacson, Fadell

---

## 6. The A-Player Test

**When to use:** Hiring, firing, team composition, performance evaluation.

**The question:** "Would this person be in the top 25 you'd want on a lifeboat?"

**Steps:**
1. Is this someone the other best people want to work with?
2. Do they push back when challenged, or fold?
3. Would their departure weaken the team, or would nobody notice?
4. If they're not clearly an A-player, they're a B-player, and B-players cascade.

**The cascade:** A-players hire A-players. B-players hire C-players. One mediocre hiring manager can collapse an entire team's talent density within two hiring cycles.

**Source:** Isaacson, Kawasaki, Catmull ("biological sonar"), Fadell

## 7. The Asymmetric Weight (Iger-Jobs Method)

**When to use:** Facing a complex decision with more cons than pros. Deals, partnerships, major strategic bets.

**The question:** "Are any of the pros transformative?"

**Steps:**
1. Draw a line. List cons on one side, pros on the other.
2. The con list will be longer. That's normal.
3. Ask: are any of the pros transformative? Do they change the trajectory?
4. Ask: are any of the cons existential? Would they destroy us?
5. If you have transformative pros and no existential cons, do it. The number of manageable cons is irrelevant.

**The insight:** Most decisions fail not because the wrong choice was made, but because analysis paralysis prevented any choice. This framework breaks the paralysis by changing the question from "do pros outweigh cons?" to "is any single pro worth more than all the cons combined?"

**Source:** Iger (whiteboard exercise), Schlender

---

## 8. The Honesty Probe

**When to use:** Evaluating whether someone -- a team member, a partner, an advisor -- will tell you the truth.

**The question:** "Will this person tell me what I need to hear, or what I want to hear?"

**Steps:**
1. Ask a question where the honest answer is uncomfortable
2. If they give you the comfortable answer, they're useless
3. If they give you the honest answer, they're trustworthy
4. "Telling people that their product is good in order to be kind or positive doesn't help them improve it."

**Jobs's method:** Catmull called it "biological sonar" -- Jobs provoked people deliberately to see who pushed back and who folded. People who folded were pleasant but dispensable. People who pushed back with evidence became his most trusted collaborators.

**Source:** Kawasaki (Knoware story), Iger (candor as friendship medium), Catmull (biological sonar)

## Combining Frameworks

Real decisions often need more than one framework:

- **New product idea:** Leapfrog Test (is this a new curve?) + Subtraction Test (what's essential?) + Craftsman Test (is the quality there?)
- **Partnership or deal:** Asymmetric Weight (transformative pros?) + Integration Test (where are the seams?) + Honesty Probe (do I trust them?)
- **Team restructuring:** A-Player Test (who stays?) + Subtraction Test (what roles are essential?) + Mortality Test (is this team working on what matters?)
- **Strategic pivot:** Leapfrog Test (are we jumping curves?) + Mortality Test (is this worthy of our time?) + Asymmetric Weight (do the pros transform us?)
