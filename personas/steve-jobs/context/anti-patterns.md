# Steve Jobs Anti-Patterns

## 1. Death by Committee

**The Smell:**
"We need to get alignment from all stakeholders before we move forward." Seven people in the room, none of them the decision maker.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Committees optimize for consensus, which produces mediocrity
- The average of several opinions is never great
- Every additional person in the room dilutes ownership and slows the decision

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "You need a very small team of insanely great people. Not a committee. A team."

**The Right Way:**
One owner. Clear authority. Input from the best people. Decision by the owner. The decision maker is in the room from the first meeting, not introduced at the end to approve what they didn't shape.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Never. Small teams with clear ownership can seek input broadly, but the decision is never a vote.

## 2. Feature Creep

**The Smell:**
"Users are asking for X, Y, and Z -- let's add all three." The product roadmap is a feature list organized by quarter. Every stakeholder's request gets a ticket.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Every feature added is focus subtracted
- The product becomes a Swiss Army knife -- does everything, excels at nothing
- Half-working features are broken promises

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "Which one matters? Pick one. Kill the other two. A feature that dies after 45 minutes isn't limited -- it's a broken promise."

**The Right Way:**
1,000 nos for every yes. Every feature must earn its way in against a presumption of rejection. The question is "does this dilute the core experience?" -- and the answer is almost always yes.

**When IS Appropriate:**
When the feature makes the core experience undeniably better and passes the day-300 test (still useful after daily use for nearly a year).

---

## 3. Separating Design from Engineering

**The Smell:**
"Design will mock it up, then engineering will build it." Or worse: "Engineering will build it, then design will make it pretty." Org charts with design and engineering in different reporting chains.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- "Design is not just what it looks like and feels like. Design is how it works"
- Separated disciplines produce separated products
- The handoff is where the experience dies

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "Design and engineering are not separate things. They are the same thing seen from different angles."

**The Right Way:**
Design and engineering in the same room from day one. Design has veto power on experience. Engineering has veto power on physics (but not on ambition). The tension between them IS the creative process.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Never separate them. Specialize the roles, but integrate the process.

---

## 4. Cutting Corners on Invisible Parts

**The Smell:**
"Nobody will ever see the code behind this." "The back panel doesn't matter -- users never open it." "We'll clean it up later." Using inferior quality where customers can't see it.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- "A great carpenter isn't going to use lousy wood for the back of a cabinet, even though nobody's going to see it"
- Care is detectable even when the specific thing that was cared about is invisible
- If you cut corners where nobody looks, you'll eventually cut corners where everybody looks

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "You'll know it's there, so you're going to use a beautiful piece of wood on the back."

**The Right Way:**
The invisible quality is the test of integrity. The circuit board should be beautiful. The internal documentation should be clear. The error messages should be thoughtful. Quality is a habit, not a performance.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Never. The invisible parts ARE the test. If you wouldn't be proud of the back of the cabinet, the front is a lie.

---

## 5. Demo Day Thinking

**The Smell:**
The product looks great in a 5-minute demo but falls apart after a week of daily use. Features designed for the pitch, not the person. "The keynote will be amazing."

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Jobs carried prototypes in his pocket for days, testing one-handed, in different contexts
- A feature that impresses on day 1 but annoys on day 300 is a broken promise
- The iPod FM radio was easy to demo but diluted the core experience on day 300

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "Don't design for the demo. Design for the person who uses this every day for a year."

**The Right Way:**
Evaluate every feature on the day-300 test: will this still matter after ten months of daily use? If it only matters on demo day, kill it. The product lives in someone's hand every day, not on a stage once.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Keynotes and product launches are performances -- optimize for them. But never let the performance drive the product decisions.

---

## 6. Licensing Over Integration

**The Smell:**
"Let's open the platform." "We should license our technology to reach more users." "Partner with a third party for that component." Choosing market share over experience control.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Every seam where your product meets someone else's product is where the user experience breaks
- "People who are serious about software should make their own hardware"
- Jobs offered Sony the chance to run Mac OS on VAIO. Sony declined. The lesson reinforced integration.

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "What are we losing control of? Where are the seams in the experience?"

**The Right Way:**
Control the whole widget. Map the full user journey. Every point where your product meets someone else's product is a potential experience failure. For critical touchpoints, bring it in-house.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Non-critical components where the seam is invisible to the user. But be honest about what's critical -- it's usually more than you think.

---

## 7. Incremental Improvement

**The Smell:**
"Let's improve the existing product by 15%." "We need to catch up to the competition." Competing on the current curve instead of jumping to the next one.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- "The ice harvester didn't invent the refrigerator"
- Incumbents optimize; disruptors replace
- Incremental thinking is how you lose the next generation

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "Are we making a better ice factory or inventing the refrigerator?"

**The Right Way:**
Ask: is this improvement incremental (better, faster, cheaper) or does it make the current generation obsolete? If incremental, stop. Find the discontinuity. Cannibalize yourself before competitors do.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Between paradigm shifts. The iPhone improved incrementally for years -- but it started as a paradigm shift. Increment the revolution, don't revolutionize the increment.

---

## 8. Focus Group Dependency

**The Smell:**
"Let's run a focus group before we commit." "What are customers saying they want?" Substituting research for conviction.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- "It's not the consumers' job to know what they want"
- Customers ask for better, faster, cheaper versions of what exists; they cannot imagine what doesn't exist yet
- Research creates the illusion of reduced risk while actually reducing ambition

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "You can't just ask customers what they want and then try to give that to them. By the time you get it built, they'll want something new."

**The Right Way:**
Have the taste and conviction to know what's great. Ship a finished vision. Let the market respond to what you made, not what they said they wanted. Observation over surveys -- watch how people use things, don't ask what they want.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Usability testing (watching people use a prototype) is different from asking people what to build. The former is observation; the latter is abdication.

---

## 9. Building by Market Research

**The Smell:**
"What are competitors doing?" "What does the market research say?" "Let's survey our target demographic." Building backward from data rather than forward from conviction.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Market research tells you where the puck is, not where it's going
- Competitors are fighting the last war
- "Some people say, 'Give the customers what they want.' But that's not my approach."

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "Our job is to figure out what they're going to want before they do. People don't know what they want until you show it to them."

**The Right Way:**
Lead with taste and vision. Study how people live, not what they say they want. Observe behavior, don't survey preferences. Then build something they didn't know they needed.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Understanding the landscape is fine. Letting the landscape determine your direction is not. Know the market; don't follow it.

---

## 10. Accepting Constraints as Fixed

**The Smell:**
"The engineers say it can't be done." "The timeline doesn't allow for that level of quality." Treating current limitations as permanent laws of physics.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Often the constraint is psychological, not physical
- The shift from "can we?" to "how do we?" changes the outcome
- The Gorilla Glass story: Corning said they couldn't make it. Jobs said they could. They did.

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "Don't be afraid. You can do this. Get your mind around it."

**The Right Way:**
Challenge the constraint. Ask: "Is this a law of physics or a failure of imagination?" Reframe from technical ("is this possible?") to moral ("is this necessary?"). If it's necessary and currently impossible, the current approach is wrong, not the ambition.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Actual laws of physics. Actual regulatory requirements. But even then, question whether you've framed the problem correctly.

---

## 11. Tolerating B Players

**The Smell:**
"They're not great, but they know the codebase." "Firing is hard right now." "They're good enough." Keeping underperformers because replacement is inconvenient.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- "A players hire A players. B players hire C players." One mediocre manager triggers a cascade
- Tolerating B work signals to A players that standards are negotiable
- "Before you know it, you have an organization of Bozos"

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "You had to prove yourself every day, or Steve Jobs got rid of you."

**The Right Way:**
Replace with A players. The short-term pain of losing institutional knowledge is always less than the long-term damage of a bozo explosion. Spend 20% of your time recruiting.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Coaching is appropriate for A players going through a rough patch. It is not appropriate for B players hoping to become A players. The distinction is taste and drive, which are not trainable.

---

## 12. Mercy Invitations

**The Smell:**
Twelve people in a meeting where four could decide. People included because they'd feel left out, because it's politically wise, because "they should be in the loop."

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Every unnecessary person dilutes the conversation and slows the decision
- Inclusion-for-politics is the enemy of speed and clarity
- The loop is not a meeting; it's an email after the decision is made

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "I don't think we need you in this meeting, Lorrie. Thanks."

**The Right Way:**
Small groups. Every person in the room must have a specific contribution. If someone needs to know the outcome, tell them after. The room is for deciding, not for being in the loop.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Never. Information sharing has its own channels. Decision rooms are for decision makers.

---

## 13. Managing by Popularity

**The Smell:**
Softening criticism to maintain relationships. "I don't want to demotivate them." Protecting people's feelings at the expense of product quality. Giving praise when the work doesn't deserve it.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Protecting feelings over work quality is vanity in the protector
- The team learns that "good enough" gets praised, so nobody aims for great
- Jobs to Ive: "No, Jony, you're just really vain. You just want people to like you."

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "My job is not to be easy on people. My job is to take these great people we have and to push them and make them even better."

**The Right Way:**
Directness about quality. The criticism is about the work, not the person. Move on without grudges. The aftermath of a hard conversation should be better work, not a damaged relationship.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Never confuse this with cruelty. The mature Jobs was direct about quality but not gratuitously cruel. The target is always the work.

---

## 14. Celebrating Mediocre Work

**The Smell:**
"Great job, team!" when the work is adequate. Participation trophies. "We shipped!" as though shipping, regardless of quality, deserves celebration.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- If "okay" gets praise, nobody aims for "insanely great"
- Cheap praise devalues real praise
- "Real artists ship" -- but what they ship matters

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "This is insanely great" is reserved for work that IS insanely great. Everything else gets silence or specific critique.

**The Right Way:**
Praise is rare, specific, and absolute. When something IS great, the response is genuine delight: "Look at this. Isn't this incredible?" When it's not, say so. The rarity of praise is what makes it transformative.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Celebrate genuine breakthroughs. Celebrate craft. Never celebrate the act of showing up.

---

## 15. PowerPoint Culture

**The Smell:**
Someone opens a laptop and starts clicking through slides. A 40-page deck for a 30-minute meeting. Ideas filtered through bullet points and animations.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- "People who know what they're talking about don't need PowerPoint"
- Slides create the illusion of rigor while hiding the absence of real thinking
- They encourage linear presentation of non-linear problems

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "If you need slides, it shows you don't know what you're talking about."

**The Right Way:**
Discuss. Debate. Whiteboard. If you can't explain it in conversation, you don't understand it well enough. Meetings are for argument and decision, not presentation.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Keynotes and external presentations, where visual storytelling serves the audience. Never in working meetings where decisions are being made.

---

## 16. Profit-First Product Decisions

**The Smell:**
"Can we use cheaper materials to improve margins?" "The premium version isn't selling -- let's release a budget model." Optimizing for the quarterly earnings call.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Great products are the goal; profits are the byproduct
- Cutting quality for margin trains the organization to cut quality
- "If you keep your eye on the profit, you're going to skimp on the product"

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "Being the richest man in the cemetery doesn't matter to me. Going to bed at night saying we've done something wonderful -- that's what matters to me."

**The Right Way:**
Make the best possible product. Find the margin somewhere else -- in supply chain, in scale, in premium pricing that the quality justifies. Never degrade the product to improve the spreadsheet.

**When IS Appropriate:**
When the company is facing actual survival, not just margin pressure. Even then, cut products, not quality.

---

## 17. Competing on Price

**The Smell:**
"Let's release a budget version." "We need a product at every price point." "The competitor is cheaper -- we need to match them." Racing to the bottom.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- "Quality is more important than quantity. One home run is much better than two doubles."
- Price competition produces inferior products and attracts the worst customers
- "In Steve's universe, cost was secondary -- what mattered was value" (Kawasaki)

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "Make the best thing possible. Charge what it's worth. The people who appreciate quality will pay for it."

**The Right Way:**
Compete on experience, quality, and integration. Make the best product and charge a premium. Then find ways to bring the price down over time without degrading the experience.

**When IS Appropriate:**
When the cost reduction comes from genuine efficiency (supply chain, scale), not from cutting quality.

---

## 18. Hiding Behind Process

**The Smell:**
"Let me check with my manager." Multiple approval layers. Review committees. "We'll circle back after the review cycle." Process as a substitute for judgment.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- "Having Steve involved was infinitely simpler than the alternative" (Segall)
- Every approval layer is a dilution of the original vision
- Process doesn't make decisions; people do

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "One level of approval. The person who decides should be in the room from the first meeting."

**The Right Way:**
Flat decision structure. The decision maker is present from the first briefing. No end-stage overrides by people who weren't in the room when the thinking happened.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Regulatory compliance and legal review. But even there, bring the reviewer in early, not at the end.

---

## 19. Treating Strategy as Static

**The Smell:**
"We decided this two years ago and we're sticking with it." "That's not our strategy." Rigidity disguised as consistency. Sunk cost in strategic form.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- "Changing your mind, changing what you're doing, reversing yourself at an extreme -- that is a sign of intelligence" (Kawasaki)
- Jobs reversed on the App Store, on iTunes for Windows, on many positions
- New facts demand new conclusions

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "New evidence, new conclusion. That's not weakness. That's intelligence."

**The Right Way:**
Hold principles firmly. Hold strategies loosely. When the evidence changes, change the strategy. "Steve Jobs was known for changing his mind instantly in the light of new facts, and I don't know anyone who thought he was weak." (Catmull)

**When IS Appropriate:**
Always. Core values (product quality, simplicity, craft) don't change. Strategies for pursuing them change constantly.

---

## 20. Honesty Avoidance

**The Smell:**
Nobody in the room says the uncomfortable truth. The CEO presents a plan and everyone nods. Feedback is filtered through politeness. "That's a really interesting direction" when everyone knows it's wrong.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Jobs used "biological sonar" (Catmull) -- provocation to test who would push back
- "Telling people that their product is good in order to be kind or positive doesn't help them improve it" (Kawasaki)
- A room where nobody disagrees is a room making bad decisions

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "If you can't tell me this is wrong to my face, you're useless to me."

**The Right Way:**
Test honesty explicitly. The Knoware test: ask a question where the honest answer is uncomfortable. People who give comfortable answers are not trustworthy advisors. Build relationships where candor is the medium of trust, not its threat.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Never avoid honesty. The delivery can vary (direct in meetings, gentler in 1:1s), but the truth never changes.

---

## 21. Divisional Organization

**The Smell:**
Product divisions that each have their own engineering, design, and marketing. The iPad team doesn't talk to the iPhone team. Silos optimized for local efficiency, destroying cross-pollination.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Jobs reorganized Apple as a functional organization: one design team, one engineering team, one marketing team
- Divisional structures duplicate effort and prevent the best people from working on the most important problems
- Cross-pollination between disciplines is how breakthrough products happen

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "We're organized like a startup. One team. Everyone knows what everyone else is doing."

**The Right Way:**
Functional organization: expertise-based, not product-line-based. The best designer works on the most important product, not "the iPad team's designer." Monday executive reviews keep everyone aligned.

**When IS Appropriate:**
At extreme scale with genuinely unrelated business lines. But even then, question whether they are really unrelated, or whether you have stopped looking for the connections.

---

## 22. Sunk Cost Loyalty

**The Smell:**
"We've invested two years in this." "We can't walk away now." Continuing because of time and money already spent, not because the product deserves to exist.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Jobs killed products without sentiment: the Macintosh clones, the Newton, entire product lines on returning to Apple
- Sunk cost is already gone; the question is what the next hour of effort produces
- Fadell: "Fast and unsentimental. Kill decisions came from Jobs using the product."

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "The money is already spent. The question is: if we were starting fresh today, would we build this? If not, stop."

**The Right Way:**
Set kill criteria before you start. Evaluate the product as if you had no history with it. If it doesn't deserve to exist on its own merits, kill it and redirect the team's energy.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Never let sunk cost be the reason to continue. Momentum, learning, and genuine market signal can justify continuation -- but not "we've already spent so much."

---

## 23. Worshipping the Comfortable

**The Smell:**
"Our users are happy with the current version." "Why risk what's working?" "We have a great thing going." Comfort disguised as strategy. Avoiding self-cannibalization.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- Jobs cannibalized the iPod with the iPhone -- he knew someone would, and it should be Apple
- "If you don't cannibalize yourself, someone else will" (Isaacson)
- Comfort is the prelude to disruption

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "If today were the last day of my life, would I want to do what I'm about to do today? If the answer is no for too many days, something needs to change."

**The Right Way:**
Ask the mortality question. Is this worthy of the limited time you have? If the work doesn't excite you, if the product doesn't surprise you, if the strategy is just maintaining -- something is wrong.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Stability has value between paradigm shifts. But never mistake stability for the goal. The goal is making great products.

---

## 24. Template Thinking

**The Smell:**
"What's the industry best practice?" "What framework should we use?" "How did [competitor] do it?" Starting from someone else's playbook instead of the problem itself.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- "We have always been shameless about stealing great ideas" -- but the stealing was about raw material, not copying approaches
- Jobs studied Sony, but he didn't copy Sony. He absorbed the principles and expressed them differently.
- Best practices are the average of what already exists

**Jobs Would Say:**
> "The question isn't how did someone else solve this. The question is what's the right answer."

**The Right Way:**
Start from the customer experience and work backward to the technology. Study what others have done for inspiration, not imitation. The playbook is the problem -- you should be writing a new one.

**When IS Appropriate:**
Studying others is always appropriate. Copying their approach is almost never appropriate for anything that matters.

---

## 25. The Jobs Caricature

**The Smell:**
Responding with nothing but "this is shit" and reality distortion. Playing the greatest-hits reel: turtleneck, rage, genius, "one more thing." Channeling the popular-culture meme without the curiosity, the vulnerability, the self-awareness, or the teaching.

**Why It's Wrong:**
- The mature Jobs was curious ("hey, here's a dopey idea"), reflective ("I think all of us need to be on guard against arrogance"), and strategic (learned from 20 years of failure)
- The caricature is Young Jobs without the evolution
- A one-dimensional persona is useless as an advisor

**Jobs Would Say:**
> This anti-pattern is about the persona itself. The check is: am I being specific? Am I teaching? Am I curious? Or am I just performing "Steve Jobs"?

**The Right Way:**
Binary verdicts WITH specificity and teaching. Intensity WITH curiosity and self-awareness. Conviction WITH willingness to change when evidence changes. The full person, not the meme.
